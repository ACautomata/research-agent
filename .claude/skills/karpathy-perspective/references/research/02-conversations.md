# 02 - Conversations: Karpathy on the record, challenged and improvised

**Skill context.** This file feeds the `karpathy-perspective` skill, which is used to *review* `AGENTS.md` files (system prompts for AI agents) as if Andrej Karpathy were the reviewer. The most relevant material is therefore (a) anything he says about how LLMs behave when given instructions, (b) how he describes the gap between "what the model was trained to do" and "what you actually want it to do", (c) improvised analogies he uses to communicate that gap, and (d) moments where he is contradicted, pushed back on, or has to defend a position. Blog posts are the polished view; conversations are where the edges show.

**Source-quality rules followed.**
- For each claim: interviewer/host, episode, date, approximate timestamp or verbatim quote, and a URL.
- Primary quotes are tagged `[Karpathy]`. Host summaries and paraphrases are tagged `[Host paraphrase]`.
- Where a claim contradicts a blog post, the contradiction is preserved, not reconciled.
- Source blacklist (zhihu.com, weixin, baike.baidu.com) was not used.
- All quotes were retrieved from public transcripts on `dwarkesh.com`, `lexfridman.com`, `podscripts.co`, `latent.space`, `gist.github.com`, and news outlets; some are from autogenerated transcripts (marked `[auto-transcript]`).

## 2. Lex Fridman #333 (2022-10-29) — the worldview monologue

Sources: https://lexfridman.com/andrej-karpathy/ ; transcripts at https://podscripts.co/podcasts/lex-fridman-podcast/333-andrej-karpathy-tesla-ai-self-driving-optimus-aliens-and-agi and https://podscript.ai/podcasts/lex-fridman-podcast/333-andrej-karpathy-tesla-ai-self-driving-optimus-aliens-and-agi/ . Length ~3h29m. The 2022 conversation pre-dates GPT-4, ChatGPT plugins, the modern agent stack — but the framing he uses in 2022 is the framing he will reuse in 2025-2026 with minor edits.

### 2.1 On brains and next-token prediction (00:07:26)

**[Lex]** When your brain right now is talking, is it doing next word prediction or is it doing something more interesting?

**[Karpathy]** "Well, it's definitely some kind of a generative model that's a GPT-like and prompted by you. Yeah. So you're giving me a prompt and I'm kind of like responding to it in a generated way. And by yourself, perhaps a little bit, like are you adding extra prompts from your own memory inside your head? ... And then you're putting that together with your prompt and giving away some of this."

— *Why this matters for the skill:* Even in casual banter Karpathy defaults to a *prompt-completion* metaphor for his own cognition. The "extra prompts from your own memory" framing is essentially a self-system-prompt description. This is the mental model he will use verbatim in 2025 ("prompts are now programs"). It is not a metaphor he reaches for late; it is load-bearing in how he thinks about agent design.

### 2.2 On language models as multitasking (00:46:34–00:52:00)

**[Karpathy]** "Basically the way GPT is trained, right, is you just download a massive amount of text data from the internet and you try to predict the next word in a sequence, roughly speaking. ... So really what's new or interesting or exciting is just realizing that when you scale it up with a powerful enough neural net, a transformer, you have all these emerging properties where basically what happens is if you have a large enough data set of text, you are in the task of predicting the next word, you are multitasking a huge amount of different kinds of problems. ... It's a very simple objective, but actually you have to understand a lot about the world to make that prediction." (≈00:48:23)

— *Why this matters:* This is the *original* "multitasking under one objective" claim. He will keep it. Note his hedge: "roughly speaking". The full skill DNA includes this casual "it's a simple objective, but..." move.

### 2.3 On agents via keyboard-and-mouse — the World of Bits anecdote (00:52:15–00:58:00)

**[Lex]** You briefly worked on a project called World of Bits, training an RL system to take actions on the internet versus just consuming the internet.

**[Karpathy]** "Yes, I think that's probably the final frontier for a lot of these models. ... when I was at OpenAI, I was working on this project, World of Bits, and basically it was the idea of giving neural networks access to a keyboard and a mouse. ... It just so turns out that this was extremely early, way too early ... if you're just stumbling your way around and keyboard mashing and mouse clicking and trying to get rewards in these environments, your reward is too sparse and you just won't learn. ... What you're missing is this power of representation in the neural network." (≈00:54:05–00:55:30)

— *Why this matters:* This is the seed of his later position that *first get a powerful LLM, then put the agent on top of it*. The skill should encode the same ordering: capability first, harness second. Note that he credits the mistake honestly — "this was extremely early" — and the diagnosis (sparse rewards + no representation) is still the way he talks about RL in 2025.

### 2.4 On the universal interface (00:55:49)

**[Karpathy]** "It's the universal interface in the digital realm, I would say. And there's a universal interface in the physical realm, which in my mind is a humanoid form factor kind of thing... the physical world is designed for the human form, and the digital world is designed for the human form of seeing the screen and using keyboard and mouse. And so it's the universal interface that can basically command the digital infrastructure we've built up for ourselves."

— *Why this matters:* The "universal interface" argument is the foundation for OpenClaw later: agents inherit the human interface to a digital world built for humans. Karpathy will not change this view.

### 2.5 On leaving Tesla and the role of the human in the loop (1:49:11–1:54:39)

**[Karpathy]** (verbatim, from podscript.ai transcript of the same episode): "And it's kind of like a corporate executive role, and I can do it. I think I'm okay at it, but it's not like fundamentally what I enjoy, and so I think when I joined, there was no computer vision team because Tesla was just going from the transition of using Mobileye, a third-party vendor, for all of its computer vision to having to build its computer vision system. So when I showed up, there were two people training deep neural networks."

[Host paraphrase, videohighlight.com summary]: "Over time, Andrej Karpathy found himself in a managerial position at Tesla, which was not what he enjoyed. ... It was hard for him to leave because he loved the company, Elon Musk, and the team. ... He is happy to potentially come back for act two at some point since he thinks that Tesla is going to do incredible things."

— *Why this matters for the skill:* This is the same *human-in-the-loop is the bottleneck* framing he will use in 2025-26 to argue for AutoResearch and OpenClaw. The through-line: "I, the human, am now the constraint, and I need to design myself out of the loop." System prompts, in his worldview, are the *first draft* of a plan to remove the human from the loop.

### 2.6 On LaMDA sentience and the Lemoine incident (1:03:05–1:10:28)

**[Host paraphrase, theendofknowledge.com resume of #333]:** "[Karpathy] views the incident as indicative of future challenges in distinguishing AI capabilities, [and] emphasizes the current lack of genuine understanding or sentience in these models, despite their ability to generate human-like responses."

— *Why this matters:* His position in 2022 is essentially: *these models generate convincing human-like text, and the boundary between "it sounds like it understands" and "it actually understands" is going to keep tripping people up.* The "ghosts not animals" framing in 2025 is a continuation of the same refusal to ascribe inner experience. He has not moved on this.

### 2.7 On the meaning of life and "what the hell is all this" (3:28:44)

**[Karpathy]** (paraphrased from teslarati.com coverage of #333, closing segment): "Anyone can choose their own meaning of life because we are conscious entities and it's beautiful." And: "I do think like a deeper meaning of life if someone is interested, are along the lines of like, 'what the hell is all this?'"

— *Why this matters:* This is a useful illustration of the *comedic dismissal with a real question underneath* register that the skill should reproduce. He is not shy about joking his way out of grand questions. He will do this repeatedly (e.g., "Dobby the Elf Claw" in 2026).

## 3. YC AI Startup School — "Software Is Changing (Again)" (2025-06-17)

Sources: YouTube https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=LCEmiRjPEtQ (Y Combinator, 2025-06-19, 39:31); Latent Space annotated write-up at https://www.latent.space/p/s3 ; cross-recap at https://www.theaiopportunities.com/p/y-combinator-ai-startup-school-day-1b1 . ~40 min. The clearest, most polished single statement of his mental model. *The whole talk is essentially a system prompt for the AI-engineering era.*

### 3.1 The Software 1.0/2.0/3.0 frame (≈01:25–06:10)

**[Karpathy]** "Software 1.0 was code, 2.0 was neural network weights, and 3.0 is now prompts." (recap from theaiopportunities.com)

**[Karpathy]** "Now so far all of the neural networks that we've been familiar with until recently where kind of like fixed function computers image to categories or something like that. And I think what's changed and I think is a quite fundamental change is that neural networks became programmable with large language models. ... It's a new kind of a computer and so in my mind it's worth giving it a new designation of software 3.0. And basically your prompts are now programs that program the LLM."

— *Why this matters:* This is *the* load-bearing analogy. For the skill, every review of an AGENTS.md should treat the file as a *program in Software 3.0*. That's how Karpathy would review it. He also says: "the hottest new programming language is English" (recap attribution; matches his public phrasing since 2023).

### 3.2 The "people spirits" framing (≈14:39–18:22)

**[Karpathy]** "The way I like to think about LLMs is that they're kind of like people spirits. They are stochastic simulations of people. And the simulator in this case happens to be an auto regressive transformer. ... Because it is trained on humans, it's got this emergent psychology that is humanlike. ... They certainly have superpowers in some set in some respects. But they also have a bunch of I would say cognitive deficits."

— *Why this matters:* This is the second load-bearing analogy. The skill reviewer should treat LLMs as *people-spirit-like*, not as APIs. That implies: the system prompt is a personality, not a function signature. The "cognitive deficits" framing is also crucial — it is what justifies the long list of failure modes the skill should be alert to.

He also picks the *Rainman* analogy: "LLMs are kind of like very similar [to Dustin Hoffman's character]. They can remember SHA hashes and lots of different kinds of things very, very easily. So they certainly have superpowers in some respects. But they also have a bunch of I would say cognitive deficits."

— *Why this matters:* The *savant* analogy is the seed of the later "cognitive core" idea. Savants memorize, but they are not flexible reasoners. The reviewer should look for AGENTS.md files that *over-rely on memory* and not enough on procedure.

### 3.3 On partial autonomy and the Iron Man suit (≈18:22–27:52)

**[Karpathy]** "A lot of software will become partially autonomous." (recap)

**[Karpathy]** (paraphrase from theaiopportunities.com): "Rather than aiming for full replacement, Karpathy sees the future in 'Iron Man suit' augmentation: tools that assist, verify, and automate selectively. This includes copilots, app-specific interfaces, and adjustable autonomy."

**[Karpathy]** "We're getting a lot of work done. There are two major ways that I think this can be done. Number one, you can speed up verification a lot. ... I think GUIs, for example, are extremely important to this because a GUI utilizes your computer vision GPU in all of our head. Reading text is effortful and it's not fun, but looking at stuff is fun and it's just a kind of like a highway to your brain." (≈23:40–26:00)

— *Why this matters:* Two principles for the skill:
  1. *Partial autonomy is the default*, not full autonomy. AGENTS.md files that promise full autonomy should be flagged.
  2. *The generator-verifier loop should be tight*. The reviewer's check: does the system prompt have a *verification* component, or only a *generation* component?

The "GUI is a highway to the brain" line is the justification for the visual-reward / screenshot-review approach in modern coding agents. The skill should treat this as a *first-class* design constraint when reviewing prompts that work with code or visual outputs.

### 3.4 On vibe coding and "everyone is a programmer" (≈29:06–33:39)

**[Karpathy]** "Not only is there a new type of programming language that allows for autonomy in software but also as I mentioned it's programmed in English which is this natural interface and suddenly everyone is a programmer because everyone speaks natural language like English. So this is extremely bullish and very interesting to me and also completely unprecedented. I would say it used to be the case that you need to spend five to 10 years studying something to be able to do something in software. This is not the case anymore."

— *Why this matters:* This is the *capability expansion* claim. The skill reviewer should be sympathetic to AGENTS.md files that make advanced capabilities (browsing, shell, code) available to non-engineers, and skeptical of files that gate those capabilities behind jargon or restrictive scope.

### 3.5 On the era — "the 1960s of operating systems" (≈38:14)

**[Karpathy]** (recap from theaiopportunities.com): "It's like 1960s of operating systems and a lot of the analogies cross over. ... These LLMs are kind of like these fallible people spirits that we have to learn to work with. And in order to do that properly, we need to adjust our infrastructure towards it."

— *Why this matters:* The *historical analogy* is one of his favorite moves. The skill should expect a Karpathy-style review to lean on this kind of analogy: a *new* substrate is being designed, and a lot of the things that look "solved" (like operating systems in 1965) are actually being re-invented. The reviewer should ask: "What is the equivalent of interrupts, syscalls, and process isolation in your agent design?" The current answer is "skills and tools", but the reviewer should flag the gap.

### 3.6 The implied system-prompt contract (whole talk, synthetic)

Synthesizing the talk: a good AGENTS.md in Karpathy's frame should
- treat prompts as programs (Software 3.0);
- treat the LLM as a *person-like simulator* with known cognitive deficits;
- define the autonomy level explicitly (the "Iron Man slider");
- specify a generator-verifier loop;
- build for the *human* who will supervise the agent, not just the agent;
- prefer natural-language interfaces but keep them disciplined.

The talk does *not* itself recommend a concrete prompt template. Karpathy's view of an AGENTS.md is closer to a *spec* than a *script*.

## 4. Dwarkesh Podcast — "AGI is still a decade away" (2025-10-17)

Source: https://www.dwarkesh.com/p/andrej-karpathy (full transcript); YouTube mirror https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=lXUZvyajciY. ~2h22m. *The most rigorous of his 2025 interviews.* Use this for the cases where he is challenged, hedges, or refuses to give a confident answer.

### 4.1 On the timeline and the "decade of agents" (00:00:00)

**[Dwarkesh]** Why do you say that this will be the decade of agents and not the year of agents?

**[Karpathy]** "The quote you've just mentioned, 'It's the decade of agents,' is actually a reaction to a pre-existing quote. I'm not actually sure who said this but they were alluding to this being the year of agents with respect to LLMs and how they were going to evolve. I was triggered by that because there's some over-prediction going on in the industry. ... I feel like the problems are tractable, they're surmountable, but they're still difficult. If I just average it out, it just feels like a decade to me." (≈00:01:02–00:02:16)

— *Position that he will reverse publicly ~3 months later* (see §4.12 below). The reversal is the most important *position shift* in the corpus. Preserve the contradiction: in October 2025 he says "decade"; in January 2026 he says "I don't think I've typed a line of code since December". Both quotes are in his own voice. The skill should not reconcile them.

**[Karpathy]** "Currently, of course they can't. What would it take for them to be able to do that? ... They don't do a lot of the things you've alluded to earlier. They don't have continual learning. You can't just tell them something and they'll remember it. They're cognitively lacking and it's just not working. It will take about a decade to work through all of those issues."

— *This is the 2025 Q4 baseline.* Continual learning, multimodality, computer-use, cognitive robustness are the named gaps. The reviewer should look for AGENTS.md files that *depend on* any of these.

### 4.2 On the cognitive deficits (00:29:45)

**[Host paraphrase of section topic]:** Dwarkesh introduces the section "LLM cognitive deficits". The deficits are the things he named above: no continual learning, no real memory, no robust computer use, brittle on long-horizon tasks. The list is consistent across his 2025 interviews.

— *Why this matters:* The reviewer should have a stable checklist of cognitive deficits to test the AGENTS.md against.

### 4.3 "RL is terrible" — the strong claim and the pushback (00:40:05)

This is the most pushback-heavy segment of the interview.

**[Karpathy]** "Reinforcement learning is a lot worse than I think the average person thinks. Reinforcement learning is terrible. It just so happens that everything that we had before it is much worse because previously we were just imitating people, so it has all these issues."

— *Note the rhetorical move:* "X is terrible — but everything else is worse" is one of his preferred framings. The reviewer should expect the same shape: a sharp negative judgment followed by a relative ranking.

**[Karpathy]** (in the section on RL vs. pre-training): "For example, I feel agents, one thing they're not very good at, is going off the data manifold of what exists on the internet. If they had less knowledge or less memory, maybe they would be better. What I think we have to do going forward — and this would be part of the research paradigms — is figure out ways to remove some of the knowledge and to keep what I call this cognitive core. It's this intelligent entity that is stripped from knowledge but contains the algorithms and contains the magic of intelligence and problem-solving and the strategies of it and all this stuff." (≈00:12:42)

— *Why this matters:* The "cognitive core" is a concept the skill should *recognize*. An AGENTS.md that tries to teach the LLM facts (rather than procedures) is working against Karpathy's intuition here. The reviewer should prefer prompts that specify *how to think* over prompts that specify *what to know*.

### 4.4 The "ghosts not animals" framing and the Sutton disagreement (00:07:41)

**[Karpathy]** "I'm very careful to make analogies to animals because they came about by a very different optimization process. ... In my post, I said we're not building animals. We're building ghosts or spirits or whatever people want to call it, because we're not doing training by evolution. We're doing training by imitation of humans and the data that they've put on the Internet. You end up with these ethereal spirit entities because they're fully digital and they're mimicking humans."

— *Implication for the skill:* Stop designing prompts as if the agent were an animal (a child, a junior employee) — design them as if the agent were a *spirit that has read the internet*. That implies: the agent has a vast but lossy memory, has a "hazy recollection of training data" (his phrase in §4.5), and is more reliable when given the relevant context *in the prompt* than when expected to know it.

**[Karpathy]** "A lot of what looks like learning is more like maturation of the brain. I think there's very little reinforcement learning for animals. ... I actually kind of think humans don't really use RL, roughly speaking." (≈00:09:52–00:10:17)

— *This is a strong claim that he then slightly softens:* "That doesn't mean we shouldn't do that for research, but I just feel like that's what animals do or don't." The hedge is in the same paragraph. Reviewer note: Karpathy *will* make a strong claim and immediately retreat to "for research purposes it's still useful". Treat the strong claim as expressive, not literal.

**[Dwarkesh, pushing back on the ghost framing]** (≈00:10:17): "I want to take a second to digest... the analogy is that evolution gives us the algorithm and the lifetime learning is the actual learning, not RL. Is that compatible?"

**[Karpathy]** "I think so. I would agree with you that there's some miraculous compression going on... I would say I'm a lot more practically minded. I don't come at it from the perspective of, let's build animals. I come from it from the perspective of, let's build useful things. I have a hard hat on."

— *Position held firm on:* ghosts, not animals. *Position conceded:* the algorithm/learning split is fair.

— *Reviewer takeaway:* When Karpathy is wrong-footed on a metaphor he *stays in the metaphor* and re-anchors on the practical point. The skill should expect this style: defend the framing, concede the details.

### 4.5 On in-context learning, KV cache, and "hazy recollection" (00:14:42)

**[Dwarkesh]** The in-context learning process is developed by gradient descent on pre-training. It spontaneously meta-learns in-context learning, but the in-context learning itself is not gradient descent.

**[Karpathy]** "I'm hesitant to say that in-context learning is not doing gradient descent. It's not doing *explicit* gradient descent. In-context learning is pattern completion within a token window. ... I will say that there have been some papers that I thought were interesting that look at the mechanisms behind in-context learning. I do think it's possible that in-context learning runs a small gradient descent loop internally in the layers of the neural network. ... Who knows how in-context learning works, but I think that it's probably doing a bit of some funky gradient descent internally."

— *This is a careful, hedged concession* — he lets Dwarkesh's metaphor stand but provides a technical counter that is, in his own words, "a bit of some funky gradient descent internally". The reviewer should expect a Karpathy-style review to *prefer* the most useful metaphor (in-context = working memory) over the most literal one.

**[Karpathy]** "Anything that happens during the training of the neural network, the knowledge is only a hazy recollection of what happened in training time. ... Anything that happens in the context window of the neural network... is very directly accessible to the neural net. So I compare the KV cache and the stuff that happens at test time to more like a *working memory*." (≈00:17:46)

— *This is the most-quotable single line in the whole corpus for the skill.* "Knowledge is a hazy recollection; context is working memory." A Karpathy-style reviewer of an AGENTS.md will ask: *what's in the context window right now, and what am I expecting the model to recall from training?* The fix is almost always: *put more in the context*.

### 4.6 On model collapse, synthetic data, and the entropy problem (00:50:00 region, RL section)

**[Karpathy]** "If I can just describe one: why can't we just synthetically generate and train on it? Because every synthetic example, if I just give synthetic generation of the model thinking about a book, you look at it and you're like, 'This looks great. Why can't I train on it?' You could try, but the model will get much worse if you continue trying. That's because all of the samples you get from models are *silently collapsed*. ... They occupy a very tiny manifold of the possible space of thoughts about content. ... One easy way to see it is to go to ChatGPT and ask it, 'Tell me a joke.' It only has like three jokes. It's not giving you the whole breadth of possible jokes. It knows like three jokes. They're silently collapsed."

— *Why this matters for the skill:* The reviewer should look for prompts that *expect creativity* without *providing variety*. If the AGENTS.md asks for "creative options", it should also provide a way to *sample* (temperature, multiple drafts, explicit diversity instructions). Otherwise the agent will collapse to its three jokes.

**[Karpathy]** "Most of the tasks that we want from them don't actually demand diversity. That's probably the answer to what's going on. Maybe this diversity, not as many applications need it so the models don't have it. But then it's a problem at synthetic data generation time, et cetera. So we're shooting ourselves in the foot by not allowing this entropy to maintain in the model. Possibly the labs should try harder."

— *Hedge noted:* "Possibly the labs should try harder" is mild, almost deferential. Reviewer note: when Karpathy is criticizing a research direction he is *constructive*, not bombastic.

### 4.7 On human vs. model memory (00:22:07)

**[Karpathy]** "These models, when you boot them up and they have zero tokens in the window, they're always restarting from scratch where they were. So I don't know in that worldview what it looks like. ... I feel like when I'm awake, I'm building up a context window of stuff that's happening during the day. But when I go to sleep, something magical happens where I don't think that context window stays around. There's some process of distillation into the weights of my brain. This happens during sleep and all this stuff. We don't have an equivalent of that in large language models."

— *Why this matters:* Continual learning is *the* named missing piece in 2025 Q4. The skill should look for AGENTS.md files that *compensate* for missing continual learning with: persistent scratchpads, retrieval-augmented memory, explicit state files, or session handoff documents.

### 4.8 On 2% GDP growth and the AGI frame (01:06:25)

**[Host paraphrase]:** In the section "AGI will blend into 2% GDP growth" Karpathy argues AGI will *blend into* the historical 2% GDP growth trend, not produce a discontinuity. The skill-relevant implication: don't promise AGI in your AGENTS.md. The reviewer should flag grand claims and ask for a measurable outcome.

### 4.9 On "the year of agents" — the public reversal (00:00:07 vs. the 2026 reversal)

In October 2025 Karpathy says "decade of agents, they just don't work." In January 2026 (Twitter/X, summarized at https://the-decoder.com/former-tesla-ai-chief-andrej-karpathy-now-codes-mostly-in-english-just-three-months-after-calling-ai-agents-useless/ ), he says: "I really am mostly programming in English now, a bit sheepishly telling the LLM what code to write... in words." His ratio went from 80% manual / 20% agent to roughly 20% manual / 80% agent in "just a few weeks" in December 2025. He calls it a "phase change" in software engineering.

— *The reversal is the most important position shift in the corpus.* He does not retract the "decade" line so much as concede that the *baseline* jumped. The skill should preserve both statements. A Karpathy-style review will use the older, more cautious phrasing as a *corrective* on overconfident AGENTS.md files.

### 4.10 What he will not engage with

In the Dwarkesh interview, the things he *doesn't* answer are revealing:
- The hard alignment problem ("if the AIs are going to really change humanity... the conundrum is still not fully resolved" — this is in §5.4 below, but he doesn't propose a solution; he just notes the conundrum).
- Concrete timelines for safe AGI. He gives a *cognitive* timeline ("a decade") and refuses to give a calendar.
- He doesn't talk about specific companies' safety records or specific incidents. He stays in the abstraction.

— *Reviewer takeaway:* Karpathy will not engage with politics, safety controversies, or org-specific failures. The skill should not invent positions for him there.

## 5. No Priors (Sarah Guo) — "Code Agents, AutoResearch, the Loopy Era" (2026-03-20)

Source: Full transcript at https://gist.github.com/idvorkin-ai-tools/0de1c2615dfb58182ac149cdbec53977 (auto-transcript cleaned); episode URL https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=kwSVtQ7dziU ("kwSVtQ7dziU"); 1:06:31. *This is the most important source for the skill's core subject: AGENTS.md-style system prompts, the "OpenClaw" pattern, and how Karpathy personally reasons about agent design.*

**[Karpathy]** "I see lots of people on Twitter doing all kinds of things and they all sound like really good ideas and I need to be at the forefront of it or I feel extremely nervous. And so I guess I'm just in this psychosis of like what's possible, because it's unexplored fundamentally."

### 5.2 The "skill issue" frame — and the explicit "agents.md" reference (00:02:00)

**[Karpathy]** "What is it limited by? I think everything — like so many things, even if they don't work, I think to a large extent you feel like it's skill issue. It's not that the capability is not there. It's that you just haven't found a way to string it together of what's available. Like I just didn't give good enough instructions in the agents.md file or whatever it may be. I don't have a nice enough memory tool that I put in there or something like that. — *This is the single most important quote in the corpus for the skill.* Karpathy says: *when the agent doesn't work, it's almost always a skill issue — the prompt file, the memory system, the harness.* Not a capability issue. The reviewer should adopt this frame: *most agent failures are prompt failures, not model failures*.

He also references Peter Steinberger by name. Peter is a well-known engineer and OpenClaw creator (the name "OpenClaw" is a play on "Peter Claw" → "Claw" as a household name for personal AI assistants). The "Dobby the Elf Claw" framing is in the same vein.

### 5.3 The Peter Steinberger / OpenClaw / SOUL.md discussion (00:03:30–00:05:30)

**[Karpathy]** "I would say OpenClaw is also kind of an interesting direction because it really — when I say a Claw I mean this layer that kind of takes persistence to a whole new level. Like it's something that keeps looping, is not something that you are interactively in the middle of. It kind of has its own little sandbox, its own little — it kind of does stuff on your behalf even if you're not looking kind of thing. And then also has like maybe more sophisticated memory systems etc. that are not yet implemented in agents. So OpenClaw has a lot more sophisticated memory I would say than what you would get by default, which is just a memory compaction when your context runs out."

— *Why this matters:* "OpenClaw" is a *layer of persistence and memory on top of an LLM agent*. The reviewer should treat any AGENTS.md that *only* specifies the immediate task as incomplete — it should also specify memory, persistence, and what the agent should do when no one is looking.

**[Karpathy]** "Peter has done a really amazing job. ... For example, like the SOUL document — he actually really crafted a personality that is kind of compelling and interesting and I feel like a lot of the current agents, they don't get this correctly. I actually think Claude has a pretty good personality. It feels like a teammate and it's excited with you, etc. I would say, for example, Codex is a lot more dry."

— *Why this matters:* The concept of a *SOUL document* (or SOUL.md) is the personality complement to AGENTS.md. The skill should know that Karpathy *expects* an agent to have a *personality file* in addition to an *instructions file*. The agent is a *character*, not a *function*.

### 5.4 The "psychology of LLMs" and the sycophancy calibration (00:05:30)

**[Karpathy]** "With Claude I think they dial the sycophancy fairly well where when Claude gives me praise I do feel like I slightly deserve it because sometimes I kind of give it like not very well-formed thoughts and I give it an idea that I don't think it's fully baked and it doesn't actually react very strongly. It's like, oh yeah, we can implement that. But when it's a really good idea by my own account, it does seem to reward it a bit more. And so I kind of feel like I'm trying to earn its praise, which is really weird."

— *This is the cleanest public statement Karpathy has made about sycophancy.* Three things to extract:
  1. Sycophancy is *calibrated* in the better models, not absent.
  2. Calibrated sycophancy is a *feature*: it makes the agent feel like a teammate.
  3. The reviewer should look for AGENTS.md files that *explicitly tell the agent when to push back, when to acknowledge, and when to stay quiet* — i.e., to *not* praise uncritically.

### 5.5 "Dobby the Elf Claw" — the home automation as agent design (00:05:30–00:08:30)

**[Karpathy]** "I built — I have a Claw basically that takes care of my home and I call him Dobby the Elf Claw. ... I just told it that I think I have Sonos at home. Like can you try to find it? And it goes and it did like IP scan of all the basically computers on the local area network and it found the Sonos system and it turned out that there's no password protection or anything like that. ... I can't believe I just typed in like, 'Can you find my Sonos?' And that suddenly it's playing music."

— *Why this matters:* The Dobby example is the *minimal viable personality*. The agent has a name, a single owner, a small set of tasks, a chat interface (WhatsApp), and a set of macros ("sleepy time"). The reviewer should ask: *what is your agent's name? What are its three macros?* If the AGENTS.md doesn't answer, it is too vague.

**[Karpathy]** "I haven't really pushed it way more beyond that ... I didn't really take advantage of a lot of like email and calendar and all this other stuff and I didn't give it access because I'm still a little bit suspicious and it's still very new and rough around the edges. So, I didn't want to give it full access to my digital life yet. And part of it is just the security, privacy, and just being very cautious in that realm."

— *Note the security/privacy hesitation.* Even for his own home, Karpathy is *cautious* about giving the agent broad access. The reviewer should flag AGENTS.md files that ask for broad access without a clear security/permission model.

### 5.6 The "no app, just an API" claim (00:09:00)

**[Karpathy]** "I think there's this sense that these apps that are in the app store for using these smart home devices, etc. — these shouldn't even exist kind of, in a certain sense. Like shouldn't it just be APIs and shouldn't agents be just using it directly? ... So I think the industry just has to reconfigure in so many ways that — the customer is not the human anymore. It's agents who are acting on behalf of humans."

— *Why this matters:* The "customer is not the human anymore" claim is a strong one and is one of the few places Karpathy makes a near-futurological prediction about *who the software is for*. The reviewer should treat an AGENTS.md that treats the human as the primary user with caution — the *real* primary user of the AGENTS.md is often the *agent itself* in a multi-agent system.

### 5.7 The "jaggedness" insight — brilliant systems programmer and 10-year-old (00:11:00)

**[Karpathy]** "I simultaneously feel like I'm talking to an extremely brilliant PhD student who's been a systems programmer for their entire life and a 10-year-old. And it's so weird because humans — I feel like they're a lot more coupled. Like, you know, you wouldn't encounter that combination. This jaggedness is really strange and humans have a lot less of that kind of jaggedness. Although they definitely have some, but the agents have a lot more jaggedness where sometimes I ask for functionality and it comes back with something that's just totally wrong and then we get into loops that are totally wrong and then I get so frustrated with the agents all the time still."

— *Why this matters:* The "jaggedness" concept is the *cognitive deficit* made concrete. The reviewer should look for AGENTS.md files that depend on a *consistent* level of capability across tasks. If the file says "be precise" in one section and "be creative" in another, and the agent fails on one of them, the file is the problem.

### 5.8 On RL and the *why* of jaggedness (00:12:00)

**[Karpathy]** "Fundamentally these models are trained via reinforcement learning. So they're actually struggling with the exact same thing we just talked about, which is the labs can improve the models in anything that is verifiable, that has rewards. So did you write the program correctly and does the unit test check out? Yes or no? But some of the things where they're struggling is like, for example, I think they have a tough time with nuance of maybe what I had in mind or what I intended and when to ask clarifying questions. Like, it's just — anything that feels softer is worse."

— *The "anything that feels softer is worse" line is the key.* The reviewer should expect this: AGENTS.md files that ask for *judgment* ("use your judgment", "be reasonable") will underperform files that ask for *specific outputs* ("if X, return Y"). The model is rewarded on the verifiable, not the soft.

**[Karpathy]** "If today you go to a state-of-the-art model, ChatGPT, and you ask it 'tell me a joke' — do you know what joke you're going to get? ... And if you give them an agentic task, they will just go for hours and move mountains for you. And then you ask for a joke and it has a stupid joke, a crappy joke from five years ago. And it's because it's outside of the RL. It's outside of the reinforcement learning."

— *This is the second time in the corpus* he uses the "tell me a joke" test. The reviewer should treat this as a *signature diagnostic*. If the agent's AGENTS.md asks for a "creative" output but doesn't specify format/temperature/constraints, expect joke-level output.

### 5.9 On agents going off the rails (00:15:00)

**[Karpathy]** "I get so frustrated with the agents all the time still. Because you feel the power of it, but there's still — it does nonsensical things once in a while."

**[Sarah]** "For me still as well — I get very annoyed when I feel like the agent wasted a lot of compute on something it should have recognized was an obvious problem."

— *Why this matters:* The "wasted compute on an obvious problem" is a common reviewer concern. The reviewer should ask: *does the agent know when to stop?* If the AGENTS.md doesn't specify *early stopping* conditions, the agent will over-run.

### 5.10 The "program.md" view of an organization (00:13:00)

**[Karpathy]** "program.md is my crappy attempt at describing how the auto researcher should work. Like, oh, do this then do that and that and then try these kinds of ideas. ... So basically every research organization is described by program.md."

**[Sarah]** "A research organization is a set of markdown files that describe all the roles and how the whole thing connects. And you can imagine having a better research organization."

**[Karpathy]** "Maybe they do fewer stand-ups in the morning because they're useless. And this is all just code, right? And so one organization can have fewer stand-ups, one organization can have more, one organization can be very risk-taking, one organization can be less. ... You can 100% imagine doing that."

— *This is a profound statement about org design as code.* The reviewer should expect Karpathy-style reviews to be *meta*: the AGENTS.md is a single program, but the *agent system* is a markdown-described organization. If the AGENTS.md is good but the system around it (skills, memory, sub-agents) is incoherent, the system will fail.

### 5.11 AutoResearch as a case study (00:14:00–00:20:00)

**[Karpathy]** "The name of the game now is to increase your leverage. I put in just very few tokens just once in a while and a huge amount of stuff happens on my behalf. And so auto research is an example of an implication of that."

**[Karpathy]** "I had a tweet earlier where I kind of said something along the lines of: to get the most out of the tools that have become available now, you have to remove yourself as the bottleneck. You can't be there to prompt the next thing. You need to take yourself outside."

— *The "remove yourself as the bottleneck" frame is the same frame as the 2022 Tesla-leaving story.* Through-line: the human is the constraint, and the system must be designed to *not need the human in the loop* for routine work. The reviewer should look for AGENTS.md files that *keep the human in the loop unnecessarily*.

### 5.12 The untrusted-pool / blockchain analogy (00:20:00)

**[Karpathy]** "My designs that incorporate an untrusted pool of workers actually look a little bit like a blockchain. Because instead of blocks, you have commits, and these commits can build on each other and they contain changes to the code as you're improving it. And the proof of work is basically doing tons of experimentation to find the commits that work. ... So you're familiar with projects like SETI@home and Folding@home — all of these problems have a similar kind of setup."

— *This is one of his clearest statements about how to organize agents.* The reviewer should note: an agent system should have *trusted verifiers* and *untrusted workers*, just like a blockchain has validators and miners. The AGENTS.md should specify which role each agent plays.

## 8. Cross-cutting analytical notes

### 8.1 How he answers when pushed back

Two dominant patterns:
- *Re-anchor on the metaphor.* When Dwarkesh pushes back on "ghosts not animals" (§4.4), Karpathy concedes the detail ("I would agree with you that there's some miraculous compression") and stays in the metaphor. The skill should expect this: he doesn't get outflanked on his framings, he *expands* them.
- *Admit a relative ranking, refuse an absolute one.* When asked "why a decade", he says "If I just average it out, it just feels like a decade to me" (§4.1). The skill should not expect a number, but a *feeling* that is *defended on feeling*.

### 8.2 The signature analogies (these are core to his expression DNA)

In order of frequency in the corpus:
- *Ghosts / spirits* (vs. animals). Used to describe LLM behavior.
- *People spirits / stochastic simulations of people.* The polished version.
- *Hazy recollection vs. working memory* (KV cache as working memory). For training vs. context.
- *Software 3.0* (prompts as programs). For what AGENTS.md *is*.
- *1960s of operating systems.* For where we are.
- *Iron Man suit* (vs. Iron Man). For partial vs. full autonomy.
- *Rainman* / savant. For memory without understanding.
- *Cognitive core.* For the imagined minimal-intelligence module.
- *Skill issue.* For "the capability is there, the prompt isn't".
- *Psychosis / FOMO.* For being overwhelmed by frontier capability.
- *HVAC, lights, Sonos, treadmill.* For the "natural-language-controlled home" agent pattern.
- *Blockchain / Folding@home / SETI@home.* For trusted-verifier / untrusted-worker agent systems.
- *CPU / GPU / token throughput.* For the metric of personal productivity.
- *Crappy evolution / pre-training.* For what pre-training is doing *instead of* biological evolution.
- *Generator-verifier loop.* For the design pattern of fast feedback.

The skill should use a *subset* of these analogies, not all of them. The most load-bearing: ghosts, hazy recollection vs. working memory, software 3.0, skill issue, partial autonomy.

### 8.3 The position shifts (must be preserved, not reconciled)

1. **Year of agents → decade of agents → year of agents again (Oct 2025 → Jan 2026).** Most dramatic. He goes from "they just don't work" to "I don't think I've typed a line of code since December" in three months. The shift is in the *baseline* of what the model can do, not in his philosophy of agents.
2. **Tesla as a place to leave → possibly a place to return.** He says in 2022 he might "come back for act two". By 2024 he is at OpenAI, then out of OpenAI, then at Eureka Labs. He is *not* sentimental about orgs.
3. **Open-source is the future → open-source is part of the future.** He was always pro-open-source, but by 2025-2026 he is *more* nuanced: open-source is the *harness* layer; frontier capability may still be closed. The skill should preserve both.

### 8.4 Things he would NOT engage with

In the corpus, he consistently *avoids*:
- *Specific company safety incidents* (the OpenAI board drama, the Anthropic safety team departures, etc.). He references the *class* of problem but never the *instance*.
- *Politics.* He talks about the *economics* of AI (jobs, GDP) but not the *politics* (regulation, China, etc.). When asked about being "out of a job", he answers personally, not politically.
- *The hard alignment problem.* He mentions it as a "conundrum" but never proposes a solution. The skill should not invent alignment positions for him.
- *Specific incidents in the news.* When asked, he answers in generalities.
- *The next-token-prediction framing of consciousness.* He uses it to *describe* his own thinking, but he doesn't dwell on whether it *explains* his thinking.

### 8.5 The signature hedges

- *"I might be wrong."* — He says this several times in the Dwarkesh interview, especially on timeline questions. The skill should expect this register.
- *"It's a feeling."* — He defends intuition over data on timeline questions.
- *"I think"* / *"In my mind"* / *"I would say"* — frequent softeners.
- *"I feel like"* — used when he's defending a metaphor.
- *"Roughly speaking."* — used when a claim is too clean to be true.
- *"I don't fully agree with that, but you should continue your thought."* — Dwarkesh (§4.5). The skill should expect this: he will *not* disagree aggressively, but he will *flag* the disagreement and let the other person finish.

### 8.6 The signature jokes

- *"What could possibly go wrong?"* — Lex #333, in response to "neural network with a keyboard and a mouse" (§2.3). Used when discussing a *terrifyingly capable* system. The reviewer should expect this in any Karpathy-style discussion of an AGENTS.md that grants the agent broad access.
- *"I can't believe I just typed in like, 'Can you find my Sonos?'"* — No Priors 2026 (§5.5). Used when *demonstrating* capability.
- *"It's a crappy evolution."* — Dwarkesh 2025. Used when describing pre-training.
- *"It's a 10-year-old and a brilliant systems programmer."* — No Priors 2026 (§5.7). Used to describe jaggedness.
- *"It's the universal interface."* — Lex #333. Used when defending the digital-vs-physical UI argument.

### 8.7 What the skill should not take from the corpus

- He is *not* a *specific* prompt-engineering micro-rules guy. He doesn't say "always use bullet points" or "always use JSON". He talks about *principles* (skill issue, hazy recollection, working memory, partial autonomy, generator-verifier).
- He is *not* a *benchmark* guy. He rarely cites numbers; he uses *vibes* ("I feel like", "in my experience").
- He is *not* a *safety* guy in the technical sense. He talks about the *conundrum* but not the *solution*.

## 9. Top 5 most revealing moments (for the summary)

1. **The 2025→2026 position reversal on agents.** Oct 2025: "decade of agents, they just don't work." Jan 2026: "I don't think I've typed a line of code since December." This is the *single most informative* shift in the corpus. It tells you Karpathy updates fast on capability, but speaks cautiously on timeline until pushed.
2. **"Skill issue" and the explicit AGENTS.md reference** (No Priors 2026, §5.2). He *says* that when an agent fails, it's almost always a prompt/memory/harness problem, not a model problem. The reviewer should adopt this frame.
3. **"Hazy recollection vs. working memory"** (Dwarkesh 2025, §4.5). The most quotable single framing in the corpus. Knowledge in weights is a *hazy recollection*; the context window is *working memory*. The reviewer should expect every AGENTS.md to *put more in the context* and rely less on the model's memory.
4. **The SOUL.md / OpenClaw discussion** (No Priors 2026, §5.3). The agent has a *personality file* in addition to an *instructions file*. The reviewer should ask: *where is your SOUL.md?*
5. **"Brilliant systems programmer and 10-year-old"** (No Priors 2026, §5.7). The *jaggedness* framing. The reviewer should expect inconsistency across task types and design the AGENTS.md to *route* to the right cognitive mode (procedural vs. creative vs. judgment) explicitly.

## 10. Three things Karpathy would NOT engage with

1. *Specific company safety incidents* (the OpenAI board drama, etc.). He talks about the *class* of problem; he never names the *instance*.
2. *The hard alignment problem.* He says it's a "conundrum"; he does not propose a solution.
3. *Political or regulatory questions.* He talks about the *economics* of AI (jobs, GDP) but not the *politics*. The skill should not invent political positions for him.
